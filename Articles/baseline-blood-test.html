What a baseline blood test tells you — and why most men don't have one.

A blood test is probably the most information-dense health investment you can make. Ten minutes, a needle, a few days, and you have a picture of what's happening inside systems you otherwise can't see.

What a good baseline covers

A useful panel for a man in midlife covers six areas: blood health, metabolic function, cardiovascular risk, hormonal status, organ function, and inflammation. Not all of these need to be repeated annually — some are more stable and warrant less frequent monitoring. But getting a baseline across all of them establishes a reference point with lasting value.

Full blood count

This measures your red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets, haemoglobin, and haematocrit. It identifies anaemia (which causes fatigue and reduced stamina), flags possible immune issues, and provides a baseline for monitoring the effects of anything that affects blood cell production — including testosterone replacement, which raises red blood cell levels and needs monitoring for that reason.

Blood glucose and HbA1c

Fasting glucose gives a snapshot of your blood sugar right now. HbA1c shows your average blood sugar over the past three months — think of it like an average speed camera rather than a point-in-time speed gun. Together, they identify pre-diabetes and type 2 diabetes. Pre-diabetes affects roughly 1 in 5 New Zealand adults, most of whom don't know it. It's reversible with lifestyle changes when caught early.

Lipid panel

Total cholesterol, LDL (the type associated with arterial buildup), HDL (protective cholesterol), and triglycerides. Most men with elevated LDL have no symptoms — the only way to know is to measure. Cardiovascular risk isn't determined by any single number but by the pattern and the context. Elevated LDL in a 42-year-old with central obesity and high blood pressure is a different picture from the same reading in a lean, active 50-year-old.

Most men with elevated LDL have no symptoms. The only way to know your cholesterol is elevated is to measure it — and the only way to act before it becomes a cardiac event is to find out early.

Testosterone and SHBG

Total testosterone and sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG), tested before 10am. If your testosterone is clearly in the normal range, you have a baseline and probably don't need to repeat it unless symptoms develop. If it's in the low-normal range, you now have a starting point for any future clinical conversation about whether it's moved.

Thyroid function

TSH and free T4. Hypothyroidism affects approximately 1–2% of men and produces fatigue, weight gain, and low mood that are frequently attributed to age or stress. It's treatable with a daily tablet. Missing it because it was never tested is unnecessary.

Liver and kidney function

Liver enzymes (ALT, AST, GGT) flag damage from alcohol, fatty liver disease, or medication effects. Kidney markers (creatinine, eGFR) assess filtration function. Both organs can be significantly impaired before producing any symptoms. Both are sensitive to alcohol and obesity in ways that accumulate quietly over years.

Vitamin D

Deficiency is common in New Zealand men, particularly in winter. It's associated with fatigue, low mood, and reduced bone density. It's cheap to test and cheap to treat. Worth including.

Why normal results aren't wasted information

If everything comes back in range, that's not a wasted test — it's a baseline. A testosterone reading of 14 nmol/L at age 42 means something different if you have another reading of 14 nmol/L at 48. If you have no prior reading, a number in isolation tells you relatively little about direction of travel. A trend is more valuable than a data point, and a data point is more valuable than an assumption.

Men who establish a baseline in their late 30s or early 40s and monitor key markers every two to three years are significantly better positioned to catch meaningful changes before they produce a clinical event — compared to men who only test when something goes wrong.

How to get one

Your GP can order a baseline panel on request. Private providers and telehealth platforms can often order blood panels without a prior GP visit. The result should be reviewed in a clinical context, not just compared to reference ranges in isolation — the same number means different things in different people, and context changes what to do with it.

A baseline blood panel is the starting point for any meaningful conversation about your health.
